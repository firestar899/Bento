1. Textual integrity

Distinct qualities, enduring value and narrative voice.

Unreliable narration is a distinct quality of the text because it develops Ono’s character while exposing the irony between how he sees himself and how others see him. Ishiguro uses this gap to dissect the human ego and its desire to protect a favourable legacy.

This is clear during Noriko’s miai, when Ono becomes egocentric and assumes that every character is judging him. He declares:

“I’m quite prepared now to acknowledge there are certain aspects to my career I have no cause to be proud of.”

The rehearsed, formal diction makes the admission sound self-important and euphemistically downplays his betrayal of Kuroda and commitment to imperialism. Setsuko then undermines him:

“No one was at all sure what father meant by it all… Father is wrong to even begin thinking in such terms about himself.”

This dramatic irony reveals how the desire for a grand legacy distorts Ono’s interpretation of the world, leading him to believe that he remains the centre of others’ judgement.

An enduring value of the text is its exploration of the human need to protect a legacy. This remains relevant because people continue to reshape their memories and public identities to defend their reputations.

During Noriko’s miai, Ono assumes that others are judging his past and announces:

“I’m quite prepared now to acknowledge there are certain aspects to my career I have no cause to be proud of.”

His elevated diction sounds rehearsed and self-conscious. Rather than fully accepting responsibility, he turns the admission into a performance that minimises his betrayal of Kuroda and support for imperialism. Setsuko’s response, “No one was at all sure what father meant by it all,” exposes the gap between Ono’s inflated sense of importance and reality.

Ishiguro therefore gives the novel enduring value by showing that ego can make individuals reinterpret the past, exaggerate their significance and resist genuine accountability.

The first-person narrative voice constructs Ono’s persona while allowing the audience to recognise the contradictions he cannot. His selective, self-protective account creates dramatic irony and makes unreliable narration central to the novel’s concerns with memory, guilt and legacy.

At Noriko’s miai, Ono announces that there are aspects of his career he has “no cause to be proud of.” The formality of his language turns apparent confession into self-dramatisation. His voice euphemistically minimises his betrayal of Kuroda and support for imperialism, while Setsuko’s later response reveals that the family did not view him as the important historical figure he imagines himself to be.

The narrative voice therefore does more than tell the story. It becomes the mechanism through which Ishiguro shows how ego edits memory, postpones responsibility and protects an idealised personal legacy.

2. Personal and intellectual connections

Post-canon insights, pivotal moments and audience.

An Artist of the Floating World offers insight into the post-canon by rejecting a single, stable version of historical truth. Through conflicting generational beliefs, Ishiguro presents the past as a mouldable narrative that changes according to the ideological needs of the present.

Suichi observes that “there seems to be no end of courageous deaths,” echoing the heroic language traditionally used to glorify wartime sacrifice. He immediately undermines this idea by adding that “they were all for stupid causes, though they never were to know that.”

The contrast between “courageous” and “stupid causes” exposes tension between the older generation’s romanticised account of war and the younger generation’s condemnation of it. The novel therefore embodies the post-canonical belief that meaning depends on perspective, and that authority over the past is contested rather than fixed.

The most pivotal moment is Ono’s attempt to meet Kuroda because it reveals the moral problem at the centre of the novel: his apparent integrity is driven by self-preservation rather than genuine remorse.

Setsuko suggests that “it is perhaps wise if Father would take certain precautionary steps.” This frames Ono’s apology as risk management, since he visits Kuroda only after learning that his past may interfere with Noriko’s marriage negotiations. Ono’s claim, “But I am a friendly visitor,” tries to construct the appearance of sincere reconciliation.

The juxtaposition creates dramatic irony between Ono’s moral façade and his actual motives. The scene is pivotal because it confirms that his apology is prompted by the threat of consequences and is designed to protect the wrongdoer’s future rather than repair harm done to the victim.

The target audience is a contemporary Western literary audience. The novel was written in English and invites readers to confront the consequences of past ideology, personal guilt and the manipulation of memory.

Setsuko’s suggestion that Ono take “certain precautionary steps” frames his response to the past as self-protection. His later claim, “But I am a friendly visitor,” attempts to create the appearance of genuine apology. The contrast exposes how individuals can perform morality when their reputation or future is threatened.

Through this dramatic irony, Ishiguro encourages contemporary readers to reflect on the difference between accountability and reputation management. The text therefore speaks to audiences living in societies that continue to revise national histories and debate responsibility for past injustice.

3. Research and findings

Methodology, literary criticism and key revelations.

I used close textual analysis, classroom materials and Google Scholar to locate academic research. This combination allowed me to test my own interpretation against narratological ideas and develop a more precise understanding of Ono’s faulty memory.

A. Azeem’s analysis helped me recognise that Ono’s narration uses moral deferral. He abbreviates shameful moments and delays revealing them, especially when discussing his betrayal of Kuroda:

“I had no idea things would go this far… I was simply doing what I conceived to be my duty.”

Gerard Genette’s concept of temporal dislocation gave me language for explaining this fragmented chronology. Remembering is not presented as a direct path to truth. Instead, Ono rearranges time to protect his ego, postpone responsibility and delay recognition of his historical guilt.

An Artist of the Floating World contributes to narratological and ethical literary criticism by questioning whether truth can be recovered from memory. Ono’s failing, selective narration becomes a form of moral deferral that delays the audience’s understanding of the harm he caused.

When he recalls Kuroda’s betrayal, Ono insists that he “had merely suggested” that someone speak with him and was “simply doing” his duty. The passive and minimising language distances him from responsibility.

Using Genette’s idea of temporal dislocation, the novel’s fragmented structure can be understood as a psychological defence. Ishiguro therefore adds to debates about unreliable narration by showing that narrative disorder is not only a technical feature. It can also be an ethical strategy through which a speaker protects a fragile ego and controls the timing of confession.

The most insightful revelation came from A. Azeem’s narratological analysis of time and memory. It helped me understand that Ishiguro does not simply portray Ono as forgetful. The novel’s delayed and fragmented revelations enact Ono’s effort to postpone ethical responsibility.

Drawing on Genette’s concept of temporal dislocation, Azeem shows how Ono abbreviates shameful memories and withholds them for as long as possible, particularly his betrayal of Kuroda. Remembering therefore becomes moral deferral rather than an honest search for truth.

This changed my reading of the novel because I began to see chronology as part of Ono’s characterisation. Ishiguro’s manipulation of time represents the way individuals restructure the past to preserve an acceptable self-image. The form of the novel therefore makes the audience experience the same delay, uncertainty and distortion that shape Ono’s understanding of guilt.
